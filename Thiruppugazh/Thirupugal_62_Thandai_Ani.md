# தண்டை அணி | Thandai Ani | திருப்புகழ் 62 | Thirupugal 62

**Author / ஆசிரியர்:** Arunagirinathar | அருணகிரிநாதர்  
**Deity / தெய்வம்:** Lord Murugan at Tiruchendur | திருச்செந்தூர் முருகப்பெருமான்  
**Song Number:** Thirupugal 62

---

## Overview

This Thiruppugazh (No. 62) is a deeply devotional song composed by the great saint-poet Arunagirinathar, addressed to Lord Murugan at the sacred temple of Tiruchendur. In this song, the poet expresses:

- A yearning for a **divine vision (darshan)** of Lord Murugan
- A description of the **beauty of Murugan's divine form and ornaments**
- A retelling of Murugan's **cosmic form (Viswarupa)** during the battle with Surapadman
- A heartfelt **plea for the Lord to appear and dance** before the devotee

---

## பாடல் | Song (Tamil)

> தண்டையணி வெண்டையங் கிண்கிணிச தங்கையுந்
> தண்கழல்சி லம்புடன் கொஞ்சவேநின்
>
> தந்தையினை முன்பரிந் தின்பவுரி கொண்டுநன்
> சந்தொடம ணைந்துநின் றன்புபோலக்
>
> கண்டுறக டம்புடன் சந்தமகு டங்களுங்
> கஞ்சமலர் செங்கையுஞ் சிந்துவேலும்
>
> கண்களுமு கங்களுஞ் சந்திரநி றங்களுங்
> கண்குளிர என்றன்முன் சந்தியாவோ
>
> புண்டரிகர் அண்டமுங் கொண்டபகி ரண்டமும்
> பொங்கியெழ வெங்களங் கொண்டபோது
>
> பொன்கிரியெ னஞ்சிறந் தெங்கினும்வ ளர்ந்துமுன்
> புண்டரிகர் தந்தையுஞ் சிந்தைகூரக்
>
> கொண்டநட னம்பதஞ் செந்திலிலும் என்றன்முன்

## Line-by-Line English Meaning

### Verse 1–2: The Music of Murugan's Anklets

**Tamil Key Words:**
- **தண்டை (Thandai)** — A type of anklet / foot ornament
- **வெண்டையம் (Vendaiyam)** — Another anklet ornament
- **கிண்கிணி (Kinkini)** — Tiny bells / jingles
- **சதங்கை (Sathangai)** — Clusters of small bells
- **தண்கழல் (Thankkazhal)** — Cool, beautiful leg ornament
- **சிலம்பு (Silambu)** — The classical anklet of Tamil culture

**Meaning:**  
The many ankle ornaments on Murugan's divine feet — the Thandai, Vendaiyam, Kinkini, Sathangai, Thankkazhal, and Silambu — all chime together melodiously. The poet recalls how Murugan once danced lovingly before His father Lord Shiva, embracing Him with affection and joy, and longs to witness that very same divine dance himself.

---

### Verse 3–4: Longing for a Vision of Murugan's Form

**Tamil Key Words:**
- **கடம்பு (Kadambu)** — Kadamba flowers (sacred to Murugan)
- **மகுடம் (Makudam)** — Crown / diadem
- **கஞ்சமலர் (Kanjamalai)** — Lotus flowers
- **செங்கை (Senkai)** — Red / lotus-like hands
- **வேல் (Vel)** — The divine spear (weapon of Murugan)
- **சந்திர நிறம் (Chandira Niram)** — Moon-like radiant glow

**Meaning:**  
The poet longs to see Murugan's complete divine form — adorned with Kadamba flower garlands, brilliant jewelled crowns, lotus-like red hands, the powerful Vel (spear), twelve eyes, six divine faces, and a radiance as cool and beautiful as the moon. He prays: *"Will Murugan not appear before me so that my eyes may be cooled and satisfied by this blessed vision?"*

---

### Verse 5–6: The Cosmic Viswarupa on the Battlefield

**Tamil Key Words:**
- **புண்டரிகர் (Pundarikkar)** — Lord Brahma (the lotus-born creator)
- **அண்டம் (Andam)** — The universe / cosmos
- **பகிரண்டம் (Bakirandam)** — The outer cosmos / the entire creation
- **வெங்களம் (Venkalaam)** — The fierce battlefield
- **பொன்கிரி (Ponkiri)** — Golden mountain (referring to Murugan's massive form)

**Meaning:**  
The poet recalls the great mythological event when Murugan went to battle against the demon Surapadman. The entire universe — including Brahma's realm and all of creation — was overwhelmed with energy and excitement. On that fierce battlefield, Murugan expanded his form to be as grand and glorious as a golden mountain, filling every direction. Even Lord Vishnu (the father of Brahma, in the metaphorical sense used here) was moved deeply in his heart at this magnificent cosmic sight.

---

### Verse 7: The Dance at Tiruchendur

**Tamil Key Words:**
- **நடனம் (Nadanam)** — Dance / divine dance
- **செந்தில் (Senthil)** — Another name for Tiruchendur
- **கந்தவேளே (Kanda Vele)** — O Lord Kanda (Murugan) with the Vel!

**Meaning:**  
The poet prays: *"O Lord Kanda! You performed that glorious divine dance at Tiruchendur. Will you not come before me now, playfully and lovingly, and dance again for this humble devotee?"*

---

### Verse 8: The Closing Prayer

**Tamil Key Words:**
- **கொங்கை (Kongai)** — Breast / bosom
- **குற மங்கை (Kura Mangai)** — Valli, the Kurava girl (Murugan's consort)
- **சந்த மணம் (Sandha Manam)** — Fragrant scent / sweet fragrance
- **கும்ப முனி (Kumba Muni)** — Sage Agastya (Agathiyar), the great Tamil sage
- **தம்பிரானே (Thambirane)** — O Lord! / O Supreme One!

**Meaning:**  
*"O Lord who is captivated by the fragrant beauty of Valli, the beloved Kurava girl! O Supreme One who is worshipped and venerated by the great Sage Agastya! Please shower your grace upon me."*

## Summary of Themes

| Theme | Description |
|---|---|
| **Divine Beauty** | A poetic description of Murugan's ornaments, especially his musical ankle ornaments |
| **Filial Devotion** | Murugan's loving relationship and dance before his father, Lord Shiva |
| **Yearning for Darshan** | The devotee's intense longing to see the Lord's complete divine form |
| **Cosmic Power** | Murugan's Viswarupa on the battlefield — a manifestation of his universal omnipotence |
| **Grace & Surrender** | A humble final plea to the Lord for His divine blessing and presence |

## Key Deities & Characters Mentioned

| Name | Identity |
|---|---|
| **முருகன் / Murugan** | The primary deity; Lord of Tiruchendur, son of Shiva |
| **தந்தை / Thanthai** | Lord Shiva, Murugan's father |
| **புண்டரிகர் / Pundarikan** | Lord Brahma, the creator |
| **புண்டரிகர் தந்தை** | Lord Vishnu (figuratively, as Brahma's source) |
| **வள்ளி / Valli** | Murugan's consort, the Kurava girl |
| **கும்ப முனி / Agastya** | The great Tamil sage Agathiyar, devotee of Murugan |

## Significance

This Thiruppugazh is particularly significant because:

1. It is associated with the **Tiruchendur Murugan temple**, one of the six sacred abodes (Arupadai Veedu) of Lord Murugan.
2. It beautifully combines **bhakti (devotion)**, **stuti (praise of the Lord's form)**, and **sharanagati (surrender)**.
3. The vivid description of Murugan's **Viswarupa** during the Surasamharam (slaying of the demon) retells a central moment in Tamil mythology.
4. The reference to **Sage Agastya** (Agathiyar) highlights the deep connection between Murugan and Tamil literature and learning.
